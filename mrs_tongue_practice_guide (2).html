Understanding Childhood Apraxia of Speech

You're doing important, loving work. Small, consistent steps can make a meaningful difference.

What is Childhood Apraxia of Speech?

Childhood apraxia of speech (CAS) is a neurological motor speech disorder. The brain has difficulty planning and coordinating the precise movements of the tongue, lips, and jaw needed for clear speech.

Your child knows what she wants to say, but the motor plan doesn’t translate smoothly to her mouth. It is not caused by muscle weakness or intellectual disability.

Mrs. Tongue’s Movement-Based Approach

Mrs. Tongue’s technique, created by speech-language pathologist Susan Dugdale, focuses on teaching the physical positions and movements the mouth needs to produce sounds.

Instead of only focusing on the sound the child hears, we help her feel and see where her tongue and lips should be. This builds the motor planning skills that are at the core of apraxia.

Core idea: Clear placement + repetition + play = stronger motor plans over time.

Why Short, Consistent, Playful Practice Works

Builds muscle memory

Frequent, brief practice strengthens the neural pathways for speech movements more effectively than long, infrequent sessions.

Keeps frustration low

Short sessions respect your child’s attention span and emotional regulation, making practice something she can enjoy.

Fits real life

5–8 minutes, several times a day is realistic for busy families and leads to better carryover than marathon sessions.

Guidance for You, Mom

You are your child’s most important practice partner. Here’s how to make home practice effective and sustainable.

Key Principles for Home Practice

Keep sessions short

Aim for 5–8 minutes. Multiple short sessions beat one long one.

Use a mirror

Visual feedback helps your child see and feel correct placement.

Keep it low-pressure & playful

Celebrate effort. Laughter and connection matter more than perfect sounds.

Prioritize consistency

Small daily practice builds skills faster than occasional intense sessions.

When Your Child Resists or Gets Frustrated

1
Change the activity — Switch to a different sound or game immediately. Don’t push through frustration.
2
Offer choices — “Would you like to do mirror faces or blow bubbles today?”
3
Make it a game — Turn practice into “Mrs. Tongue’s adventure” or treasure hunt.
4
End on a win — Finish with something she does well or enjoys. Leave her wanting more.
5
Take a break — It’s okay to pause for the day. Tomorrow is a new opportunity.
Remember: Some days will be harder than others. That doesn’t mean you’re doing anything wrong. Your calm, consistent presence is what matters most.

Tongue & Lip Positioning

Clear placement is the foundation. Practice these positions together in front of a mirror so your child can see and feel what her mouth is doing.

Lips Together

p • b • m

Gently press your lips together. For p and b, release with a small puff of air. For m, keep lips closed and let sound come through your nose (hum).

Mirror Tip:

Watch your lips come together and then open. Try making a kissing motion first, then add the sound.

Tongue Tip Behind Upper Teeth

t • d • n

Place the very tip of your tongue just behind your upper front teeth (the bumpy ridge). For t and d, tap and release quickly. For n, keep the tongue in place and let air flow through your nose.

Mirror Tip:

Look in the mirror and see the tongue tip touch the spot right behind the teeth. It should feel like a gentle “tap” for t/d.

Back of Tongue Up

k • g

Raise the back of your tongue so it touches the soft part of the roof of your mouth (velum). For k and g, let it “pop” off quickly with air.

Mirror Tip:

This one is harder to see. Feel the back of your tongue rise. You can practice saying “ah” then lifting the back to make a “k” sound.

Sound Practice

Focus on one sound group at a time. Use the mirror, then move into words and play.

Tip: Click the Listen button to hear the sounds and words spoken clearly. Listen together first, then have your child watch your mouth in the mirror and try to copy the placement and sound. The computer voice may not have perfect natural intonation, but it’s clear and consistent for practice.
Why focus on these sounds first?

These three placements — lips together, tongue tip behind the teeth, and back of the tongue — are the building blocks for many early words. They are the most visible in a mirror and easiest for young children to feel and control.

Other sounds (such as /s/, /f/, /sh/, /l/, /r/, and most vowels) involve finer or more complex tongue movements. We start here to build strong motor plans and early success. Your child’s speech-language pathologist will guide when and how to expand to the full range of sounds based on her progress.

Activities & Games

Keep practice fun and embedded in play. Here are simple, effective activities you can do at home.

Mirror Magic

Sit together in front of a mirror. Make exaggerated faces and movements together — big smiles, kisses, tongue taps. Narrate: “Look how Mrs. Tongue goes up!”

Bubble Time

Blow bubbles together. Practice “p-p-p” and “b-b-b” as you pop them or blow more. Great for lip rounding and controlled airflow.

Animal Sounds

Use animal sounds with clear mouth movements: “mmmmm” (cow), tongue clicks, “k-k-k” (chicken). Make it silly and joyful.

Treasure Hunt

Hide small toys or stickers. Each time your child finds one, she says a target word or sound to “unlock” the treasure.

Song & Rhyme Time

Sing simple songs that repeat target sounds (“Pat-a-cake”, “Row Row Row Your Boat”, or made-up rhymes). Movement + rhythm helps.

Mrs. Tongue Story Time

Use Susan Dugdale’s printable story. Act out the movements together as you read. The story makes placement memorable and fun.

A Realistic Daily Rhythm

You don’t need a rigid schedule. The goal is 3–5 short moments spread through the day. 5–8 minutes each is enough.

Morning

5 minutes while getting dressed or at the breakfast table.

Quick mirror faces + 2–3 target words before the day gets busy.

Afternoon

5–8 minutes during playtime or after nap.

One focused activity (bubbles, treasure hunt, or story time).

Evening

5 minutes as part of the bedtime routine.

Calm mirror time or reading with gentle sound emphasis before bed.

Flexible & forgiving

If one session gets skipped, that’s okay. The consistency of showing up most days matters far more than perfection. Listen to your child’s energy and adjust.

Appendix: Working Toward the Full Alphabet

While the early placements (lips together, tongue tip, back of tongue) are the foundation we focus on first, many children with apraxia of speech eventually work on the complete set of English sounds — including later consonants and all the vowels.

Later-Developing Consonants

These sounds generally require more precise tongue shaping, airflow, or coordination. They are often introduced after the foundational placements are stronger.

Fricatives (airflow sounds)
f / v — lip to teeth (fan, van)
s / z — tongue tip near teeth with hissing (sun, zoo)
sh / zh — lips rounded, tongue blade (shoe, measure)
th (voiced & voiceless) — tongue between teeth (this, think)
Affricates & Other
ch / j — quick stop + fricative (chair, jump)
l — tongue tip touches alveolar ridge with sides open (light, ball)
r — complex tongue bunching or retroflex (red, car) — often one of the last
h, w, y — glides (hat, water, yes)

Vowels

Vowels are “open” sounds shaped by the position of the tongue (high / mid / low and front / back) and whether the lips are rounded or spread. Unlike consonants, there is no complete contact or blockage of airflow.

Because they require sustained shaping and smooth transitions rather than clear “placements,” vowels are often practiced through:

  • Sustained single vowels (“ahhh”, “eeee”, “oooo”)
  • Simple consonant + vowel combinations (ba, bee, boo, bye)
  • Songs, animal sounds, and exaggerated play
Short Vowels (examples)
/æ/ — cat, apple, hat
/ɛ/ — bed, egg, head
/ɪ/ — sit, it, fish
/ʌ/ — cup, up, sun
/ɑ/ or /ɒ/ — hot, on, dog
Long Vowels & Diphthongs (examples)
/iː/ — bee, eat, see
/eɪ/ — day, play, cake
/aɪ/ — my, high, bye
/oʊ/ — go, no, boat
/uː/ — moon, blue, shoe

Diphthongs are gliding vowels (the mouth moves during the sound).

Important note: Every child’s sound system develops in a unique order. Your speech-language pathologist will create a personalized plan based on your daughter’s current strengths, motor planning needs, and functional communication goals. The appendix above is for general information only.
